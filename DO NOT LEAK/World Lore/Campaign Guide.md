# Part One: The World Beyond the Isles

Before the Accord, before the Isles mattered to anyone but the people living on them, the gods of Faerûn concerned themselves with the Sword Coast, with Calimshan, with Thay and the Dalelands and all the great theaters of mortal struggle. The Isles of Ghandara were peripheral. Months away by ship, culturally distinct, home to syncretic religious traditions that mainland theologians found quaint. The gods had faithful here, certainly, but the Isles were never the center of anything.

That changed because of Lliira.

## Why the Gods Are Here

The gods of Faerûn draw power from worship. Not exclusively—they are beings of immense cosmic authority regardless—but worship *sustains* them, shapes them, keeps them relevant in the mortal sphere. The more faithful, the more fervent, the stronger the god's ability to act in the world.

For most of history, this worked through a kind of divine free market. Gods competed for worshipers through doctrine, miracle, and tradition. Temples rose and fell. Faiths waxed and waned. The balance was imperfect and often violent, but it was *dynamic*. Mortals chose their gods. Gods, in turn, served their mortals—or lost them.

Bane saw the flaw in this arrangement. Or rather, Bane saw the *opportunity*.

### The Divine Accord — Why It Happened

Forty years ago, Bane proposed an alliance to Helm and Tyr. His argument was elegant, calculated, and partially true:

**To Helm:** "Mortals are destroying themselves. Wars, plagues, the Spellplague's aftermath—they cannot protect themselves. You know this. You've watched them suffer because they refuse guidance. The Accord would let you *actually protect them*, not just stand watch while they bleed."

**To Tyr:** "Justice cannot function without law, and law cannot function without enforcement. Mortal governance is corrupt, fragile, and inconsistent. Divine law—codified, universal, enforced by beings who cannot be bribed or broken—would create true justice for the first time in history."

**What Bane actually wanted:** Power. Consolidated, permanent, unchallengeable power. If the gods stopped competing and started cooperating under a unified structure, the faithful had no alternatives. Worship becomes compulsory. And whoever *architects* that structure controls it.

Helm agreed because he genuinely believes mortals need protection from themselves. His participation is sincere, and that sincerity is the Accord's most powerful weapon—because when the most trusted guardian in the pantheon says "this is right," people believe him.

Tyr agreed because the legal logic was sound. If divine law supersedes mortal law, and the gods are just, then divine governance *should* produce better outcomes. Tyr's tragedy is that he's correct in theory and catastrophic in practice. Just laws unjustly enforced are still unjust.

Once the three Architects aligned, other gods faced a choice: join or resist. Most joined. Some because they believed (Sune told herself beauty requires order). Some because they were afraid (Lathander saw what was coming and couldn't stop it). Some because they saw opportunity in the new order (Shar, Loviatar, Cyric slid into the Accord's shadow like parasites into a wound).

The Accord was established on the Sword Coast first. Within a decade, it had spread to every major continent. The gods had reorganized the fundamental relationship between divine and mortal.

### Why the Isles of Ghandara Matter

The Isles were supposed to be irrelevant. Too far, too small, too strange. The Accord would get to them eventually, but they weren't a priority.

Then Lliira made them the most important place in the world.

Lliira, goddess of joy and freedom, opposed the Accord from the beginning. She spoke against it publicly in the divine courts. She encouraged her faithful to resist. She was, by any measure, the Accord's most vocal opponent among the gods—not the most powerful (Tymora and Selûne had more resources), but the most *visible*.

And her faithful gathered every decade at Joybright.

**Joybright** was Lliira's greatest holy city, built on the western coast of Ghandara's main island. Every ten years, Lliirans from across Faerûn made pilgrimage here for the **Festival of Endless Joy**—weeks of celebration, dance, music, and worship that reaffirmed everything the Accord was trying to suppress. Joy as a communal act. Freedom as a form of prayer. The idea that happiness itself was sacred and could not be legislated away.

The Accord couldn't tolerate this. Not because a festival threatened their military control, but because *joy is contagious*. People who've experienced genuine communal celebration don't go home and meekly submit to divine law. They remember what freedom felt like. The Festival of Endless Joy was a recruitment engine for dissent, running every decade, openly, in a place the Accord couldn't easily reach.

Twenty-three years ago—seventeen years after the Accord's founding—Lliira's faithful gathered for the Festival. And the Accord came to Joybright to end it permanently.

## The Execution of Lliira

The sky above Joybright became a stage. The Accord wanted witnesses. They wanted the world to see.

**Bane** pronounced sentence. Sedition against divine order. Incitement of mortal disobedience. Refusal to submit to the consensus of the pantheon. His voice rolled like thunder across the sky, audible for miles, and his words were *legal*—precise, procedural, devastating. This wasn't murder, according to Bane. This was justice.

**Helm** restrained her. He held a goddess of joy and freedom so she could not flee, could not fight, could not escape. He did this because he was commanded to. Because the Accord required it. Because duty, to Helm, supersedes love, conscience, and doubt. This is his greatest virtue. It is the wound at the center of his faith.

**Loviatar** administered the killing blow. Slowly. The Maiden of Pain took her time because that is her nature, and because the Accord wanted the faithful below to *watch*. To understand that even their goddess could suffer, could break, could die. Loviatar remembers this as her finest work.

**Lathander** was present. Witnesses disagree whether he watched in chains or stood freely among the executioners. The truth is worse than either story: he stood freely, but he could not look away. Something in the Morninglord dimmed that day and has not recovered.

Lliira did not beg. Some say she laughed. Some say she wept for those watching. Her divine spark was extinguished. Her body became light, then ash, then nothing.

The city of Joybright didn't sink that night, as some tales claim. But something in it *broke*. The eternal music stopped. The festival lights went out. Within a year, the city was empty—not destroyed, just abandoned, as if joy itself had left and there was no reason to stay.

Joy has not felt the same since. Festivals exist across the world but ring hollow. Laughter in public draws glances. The color scarlet—Lliira's sacred hue—is quietly forbidden.

## The Slow Tightening (23 Years Ago to Present)

This is crucial to understand: the Accord didn't conquer Ghandara overnight. The tightening has been gradual, deliberate, and designed to feel inevitable rather than imposed.

### Phase One: The Message (23-20 Years Ago)
Lliira's execution was the statement. The Accord didn't send troops immediately. They didn't need to. The message was clear: even gods kneel or die. Word spread across the Isles. Some faithful hid their shrines. Some burned their own symbols preemptively. Fear did most of the work.

### Phase Two: The Missionaries (20-15 Years Ago)
Helmite missionaries arrived first. Not soldiers—*missionaries*. They built temples. They offered protection. They healed the sick and guarded the vulnerable. They were everything Helm's faithful had always been: brave, selfless, and absolutely sincere. They made people *want* to worship Helm. The Tyrrans followed with courts that resolved disputes fairly and punished actual criminals. For a time, the Accord looked like progress.

### Phase Three: The Administration (15-10 Years Ago)
Once the temples and courts were established, the laws changed. Slowly. Worship of "unapproved" gods wasn't banned outright—it was *regulated*. You needed a license. You needed to register your shrine. The registration forms asked for names and addresses. The information went into files. Files were reviewed.

Helmite paladins transitioned from missionaries to enforcers. Checkpoints appeared. Harbor inspections became routine. Contraband lists expanded. The Tyrran courts began hearing cases against "unlicensed worship." The sentences were light at first. Fines. Community service. Confiscation of materials.

Then the sentences got heavier.

### Phase Four: The Crackdown (10-5 Years Ago)
Banite enforcers arrived. Not many—a handful of advisors, a small garrison in Accord's Rest, an "observer" here and there. But their presence changed everything. The Helmites had been police. The Tyrrans had been judges. The Banites were *inquisitors*. They didn't patrol or hear cases. They *hunted*.

Dissenter shrines were raided. Families were separated—children of "unfit" worshipers taken to "proper" institutions. The Compliance Ministry was established: a covert branch using agents like bards, merchants, and travelers to identify and neutralize dissent before it organized.

Tessa Brightwell's husband Marren was arrested during this phase. Possessing a Book of Exultation. Ten years ago. She doesn't know if he's alive.

### Phase Five: The Present (5 Years Ago to Now)
The grip has tightened to the point where even compliance feels insufficient. It's not enough to worship approved gods—you must worship them *correctly*. It's not enough to avoid dissent—you must actively demonstrate loyalty. Public oath ceremonies. Mandatory attendance at approved services. Informant networks.

And Session 1 happens *now*, at the moment the Accord decides that Lliira's final execution—the destruction of Joybright itself, or the killing of something that survived—represents the final chapter. The crackdown is becoming a purge.

# Part Two: The Divine Players

## The Architects — True Believers

### Bane — The Iron Voice
*God of tyranny, fear, and hatred.*

Bane is the Accord's architect in the truest sense. He conceived it, he argued for it, and he benefits most from it. But reducing Bane to simple villainy misses what makes him dangerous: he has a *vision*. Bane genuinely believes that mortal free will produces suffering. Democracy produces corruption. Freedom produces chaos. Only absolute authority—wielded by beings incapable of the weakness that afflicts mortals—can create lasting peace.

He's wrong. But he's *coherent*. And coherent evil is harder to fight than senseless cruelty.

**What Bane wants:** Total, permanent control of the mortal sphere. Not just worship—*dependency*. A world where mortals cannot conceive of existence without divine authority. The Accord is his mechanism, but it's also his cage: he must share power with Helm and Tyr, who limit his worst impulses. He's patient. He'll erode those limits over time.

**His faithful in Ghandara:** The Black Fists. Small in number but growing. They serve as inquisitors, interrogators, and symbols of what happens when the Accord stops being polite. Their ranks include Fists (enforcers), Dread Knights (paladin-hunters), Thought-Takers (clerical mind-readers), and Autarchs (regional inquisitor-commanders who can override local Helmite and Tyrran authority).

**His plan:** Bane is maneuvering to make the Banite presence in Ghandara equal to the Helmite presence. He's sent Inquisitor Malrek Thorne to Accord's Rest as the vanguard. If Thorne succeeds—if he finds what the Accord fears survived of Lliira—Bane will use it to justify a permanent Banite garrison and begin the process of sidelining Helm entirely.

---

### Helm — The Dutiful Hand
*God of guardians, protectors, and vigilance.*

Helm is the Accord's most tragic figure. He is not evil. He may not even be wrong about the need for protection. But he has allowed his greatest virtue—duty—to become the instrument of oppression. He held Lliira while she died. Not from cruelty, not from hatred, but because he was ordered to, and Helm does not abandon a charge.

**The wound at his center:** Helm's faithful believe duty is sacred, that obedience honors trust, that protection requires strength. Before the Accord, Helmites were bodyguards, castle wardens, sentinel knights. A Helmite at your door meant you were safe. Now a Helmite at your door might mean you're under arrest.

**What Helm wants:** Genuinely, sincerely: to protect mortals. He believes the Accord accomplishes this. But something in him is *unsettled*. He held a goddess of joy while she was tortured to death. If that was righteous, what does righteousness mean? If it wasn't—if even Helm can be made to serve cruelty—then what is the Order actually protecting?

**His faithful in Ghandara:** The dominant enforcement presence. Chapterhouses in every significant settlement. The Unsleeping Bastion in Kell's Landing. The High Citadel in Accord's Rest. Professional, disciplined, rarely cruel but utterly uncompromising. Commander Serelith Vane, Brother Aldric, Sister Maren, Brother Fell—all represent different points on the spectrum from conviction to doubt.

**The High Watcher:** Varanthas Morn, supreme authority of the Helmite faith. Present at Lliira's execution. Has not smiled since. Whether this reflects doubt or satisfaction, no one knows.

**Helm's secret:** Commander-General Aldric Vorn has heard Helm's voice exactly twice. The second time, three years ago, it said only two words: *"I'm sorry."*

---

### Tyr — The Legal Framework
*God of justice, law, and righteousness.*

Tyr was convinced through cosmic jurisprudence that the Accord is lawful. His argument: if divine beings are inherently more just than mortal beings (debatable but axiomatic in Tyr's framework), then divine governance should supersede mortal governance wherever they conflict. The Accord's laws are technically sound. The trials are procedurally correct. The sentences follow from the statutes.

The problem is that just laws unjustly applied are still unjust. And laws designed to suppress freedom are unjust by nature, no matter how carefully they're administered.

**What Tyr wants:** A universe governed by perfect law. He believes the Accord moves toward this. He is increasingly troubled by the *application* of that law—by Banite excess, by Helmite rigidity, by the gap between his legal theory and the suffering it produces. But Tyr cannot admit the framework is flawed without admitting *he* is flawed, and the god of justice admitting injustice would be an existential crisis.

**His faithful in Ghandara:** The Tyrran Courts. Magistrates in every major settlement. They run trials, pass sentences, and provide the Accord with legal legitimacy. Ranks include Justicars (local judges handling minor infractions), Magisters (regional authorities for serious cases like sedition and heresy), High Arbiters (the senior judiciary in Accord's Rest who hear appeals and set precedent), and the Blind Council (supreme legal body, blindfolded to represent impartiality—dissenters call this ironic).

**How the courts work:** A person accused of dissent is arrested by Helmites, tried by Tyrrans, and if convicted, sentenced according to statute. The process is meticulous. Evidence is weighed. Witnesses are heard. The accused may speak in their defense. And then they are convicted, because the laws are written so that any deviation from approved worship is criminal by definition. The process is fair. The outcome is predetermined. This is Tyr's tragedy.

**Sentencing guidelines:**
- Unlicensed worship: Fine, confiscation of materials, mandatory attendance at approved services
- Possession of forbidden materials: Imprisonment (months to years), property seizure
- Active dissent (organizing, recruiting, sheltering): Extended imprisonment, forced labor, family separation
- Sedition (inciting rebellion, attacking Accord personnel): Public trial, execution or life imprisonment
- Heresy (claiming divine authority outside the Accord): Death

## The Opportunists — Parasites in the Wound

### Shar — The Whispering Dark
*Goddess of darkness, loss, and despair.*

Shar doesn't enforce the Accord. She *feeds* on it. Every crushed hope, every hollow festival, every person who stops believing things can get better—that's worship to Shar, whether they know it or not. She has embedded Sharran operatives in communities across Ghandara: "grief counselors" who befriend the despairing and note who still has enough spirit to be dangerous. Informants who cultivate hopelessness to weaken resistance. Secret police who report to no one the Accord officially acknowledges.

### Loviatar — The Pain Artist
*Goddess of pain, suffering, and torment.*

Loviatar administered Lliira's killing blow. Slowly. She remembers it as her finest work, and she's been chasing that high ever since. Her faithful serve as "specialist interrogators" when Banite methods prove insufficient. They are called in for prisoners who hold something truly valuable and won't break under conventional pressure. The Loviatans are artists. They take their time.

### Cyric — The Lie Weaver
*God of lies, deception, and murder.*

Truth is dangerous to the Accord. Cyricans ensure that truth is hard to find. They pose as fellow dissent ers to identify real ones. They spread false rumors about safe houses and resistance movements. They forge documents creating evidence of crimes that never happened. The Compliance Ministry—the covert Accord branch that sent Nessandrle after the party—operates with heavy Cyrican influence.

## The Complicit — Gods Who Fell in Line

### Sune — Beauty in Chains
*Goddess of beauty, love, and passion.*

"Beauty requires order," she tells herself. Her temples still stand. Her services still occur. But something is hollow. Sunite priests preach that beauty requires structure, that chaos mars perfection. They host approved gatherings—elegant, restrained, *joyless*. The strange temple on Miravel that's become "strangely influential" may be Sune's faithful trying to preserve something real beneath the Accord's veneer.

### Lathander — The Dimmed Dawn
*God of dawn, renewal, and vitality.*

The Morninglord was present at Lliira's execution. Whether he watched in chains or stood freely among the executioners, something in him *broke*. His temples are quiet. His dawn services are muted. His priests speak of renewal and hope, but their eyes say otherwise. Lathander is the Accord's most reluctant collaborator—and the one most likely to defect if given reason.

### Eldath — Peace at Any Price
*Goddess of peace, quiet places, and still waters.*

Eldath weeps but does not act. Peace at any cost means accepting injustice. Her rural shrines are marginal, tolerated, ignored. Her faithful are the gentlest of all the gods' servants, and the Accord considers them harmless. This may be a mistake. Still waters run deep.

## The Dissenters — Those Who Refuse

### Tymora — The Furious Friend
*Goddess of luck, fortune, and adventurers.*

Lliira was Tymora's closest friend. She is *furious*. Reckless. Funding mortal rebellions through luck and happenstance. Her faithful—gamblers, risk-takers, the desperate and the bold—operate through "coincidence." A guard's boot catches on a cobblestone at the right moment. A key falls from a pocket. A storm blows a ship off course and toward freedom. Three-Coin Mira in Kell's Landing runs her resistance funding through Tymoran "luck."

### Mask — The Shadow Network
*God of thieves, shadows, and intrigue.*

Authority is meant to be undermined. Mask runs smuggler networks and spy cells across the Isles. The Beneath—the undercity network beneath Accord's Rest—is his masterwork. The Whisper, whoever they are, operates with Mask's blessing. Mask doesn't care about ideology. He cares about freedom from control, which makes him the Accord's most practical opponent.

### Selûne — The Patient Moon
*Goddess of the moon, change, and cycles.*

This stagnation is an abomination. Selûne works in cycles, in patience. Her faithful gather secretly on full moons, watching, waiting. The Moon Circle operates from Selûne's Veil and hidden cells across the Isles. They believe change is coming—not through force, but through the turning of a cycle that even the Accord cannot stop.

### Eilistraee — The Already Outcast
*Goddess of the drow who walk in moonlight.*

Already marginalized, already fighting. Her faithful move through the underdark and surface both. They have experience with being hunted that the other dissenters lack. In Ghandara, Eilistraeean agents may operate in places no one else can reach.

### Ilmater — The Enduring Question
*God of suffering, martyrdom, and endurance.*

Ilmater occupies the strangest position of all the dissenters. He will not cause suffering—he *cannot*, it violates his fundamental nature. He accepts his own punishment rather than enforce the Accord. His temples remain open, his faithful unpersecuted, because the Accord considers them harmless. Passive. Broken.

They may be wrong about that.

**The Ilmatari in Ghandara:** Brother Aldous tends the faithful in Autumnvale, where Wolfric was raised among twelve siblings. The Church of Enduring Light preaches patience, compassion, and endurance. The Accord watches but hasn't moved against them. Why would they? The Suffering God's faithful just... suffer. They don't fight. They don't organize. They don't resist.

Except some of them do. Quietly. They call themselves **the Unbroken**. Their theology is simple: suffering should be endured, not inflicted. The Accord inflicts suffering constantly. Therefore, opposing the Accord doesn't violate Ilmater's teachings—it fulfills them. They don't fight directly (that would cause suffering). They shelter. They heal. They spirit people away from Accord justice. And they're waiting for a sign from their god that the time for patience has ended.

**The keeper of the Broken Tower** in Kell's Landing is part of this network. Murdock swore his oath to Ilmater there.

**For Wolfric:** The central question of his faith. Is endurance the same as acceptance? Is patience the same as complicity? When Wolfric and Murdock prayed at sea and felt Ilmater's warmth, his *presence*—what was the god saying? Keep waiting? Or *the wait is over*?

### The Wild Cards

**Kelemvor** — Neutral by duty, god of the dead. But Lliira's soul passed through his realm when she died. Something *changed* in him. He hasn't acted, but he hasn't forgotten. What does the god of death think about a death that was fundamentally *wrong*?

**Mystra** — Magic is meant to be free. The Accord restricts magical practice, regulates spellcasting, controls access to arcane knowledge. Mystra hasn't openly resisted, but there are rumors of mortals suddenly manifesting powers they shouldn't have. Is she seeding the world with wild magic, preparing weapons the Accord can't confiscate?

# Part Three: The Island of Ghandara

The main island—the largest landmass in the archipelago, and the seat of everything that matters. Home to Accord's Rest, the ruins of Joybright, the Four Vales, the highland clans, and approximately 185,000 souls living under varying degrees of divine authority.

## At a Glance

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Total Population** | ~185,000 |
| **Capital** | Accord's Rest (pop. ~45,000), formerly Mirahaven |

## The Regions

### The Northern Reaches
*Cold, remote, frontier territory. The Accord's grip is weakest here.*

Dense forests, rocky coasts, long winters. The great Thornwood dominates the interior. Travel is difficult, resources are scarce, and the Accord has never committed sufficient forces to truly control the region.

| Settlement | Pop. | Description |
|------------|------|-------------|
| **Northern Rest** | ~1,500 | End-of-the-world fishing town. Last stop before open sea. A single elderly Helmite "keeps the peace" by being ignored. Smugglers use it as a waypoint. |
| **Springvale** | ~4,000 | The "loyal" vale, positioned on the main northern road. Helmite waystation, Tyrran toll collectors. Locals comply, teach their children to smile at patrols. |
| **Frostholm** | ~600 | Remote village near the northern passes. Ice fishing, fur trapping. Technically under Accord law; practically ungoverned. |
| **Northwatch Tower** | ~40 | Accord watchtower on the northern coast. Lonely punishment posting. The garrison drinks heavily. |

**Notable:** The Oldgrove (druid forest, surveys fail), Frostmere (frozen lake, something sleeps beneath), The Warden's Stones (pre-human standing stones, locals leave offerings).

### The Briarholt Expanse
*Dense forests, logging industry, and secrets among the trees.*

The central-eastern region dominated by the Thornwood. Economically vital—thornwood timber builds ships across the Isles. The Accord watches closely, but forests are hard to patrol.

| Settlement | Pop. | Description |
|------------|------|-------------|
| **Briarholt** | ~12,000 | Second city of Ghandara, logging capital. Merchant guilds hold real power; Accord governs on paper. Timber Guild arbitration settles most disputes. Dissenter sympathizers in the camps. |
| **Death's Edge** | ~800 | Mining town on northeastern cliffs. Iron, copper, and something else. Workers die at uncomfortable rates. Banite overseer arrived last year. |
| **Millford** | ~1,200 | River town for timber processing. Sawmills run day and night. Workers underpaid and resentful. |
| **Verdant Crossing** | ~900 | Trading post at the forest road junction. Tilla Bramblefoot (halfling) runs the inn and knows everyone's business. |
| **Ashwick** | ~450 | Charcoal-burning settlement. Rough workers, Timber Guild oversight. Don't ask about travelers passing through. |
| **Hollowbrook** | ~350 | Officially a woodcutter's camp. Actually a dissenter waystation. |

**Notable:** Whisper's Hollow (abandoned village, Pacification sealed records, buildings preserved as if inhabitants vanished), The Guildmaster's Lodge (secret meeting place, location changes), The Scarred Oak (ancient tree with lightning scar resembling a laughing face).

### The Accord's Heart
*The capital region. Order, law, and the iron grip of divine authority.*

The Accord's showcase. Prosperous, orderly, thoroughly controlled. Dissent here is invisible.

| Settlement | Pop. | Description |
|------------|------|-------------|
| **Accord's Rest** | ~45,000 | The capital, formerly Mirahaven. High Citadel of Helm, Hall of Scales (Tyrran courts), Black Fist Garrison (Banite, growing). Beautiful architecture, hollow festivals, fear dressed as piety. |
| **Helmgate** | ~3,500 | Fortified checkpoint on the northern road. Travelers inspected, questioned, documented. |
| **Piety's Rest** | ~2,000 | Monastery-town. Religious schools and "re-education" facilities. Some who enter don't emerge. |
| **Goldfield** | ~2,800 | Agricultural estates supplying the capital. Wealthy Accord-approved landowners. Stark inequality. |
| **Watchward** | ~1,500 | Military garrison town. Helmite training, armories, barracks. |
| **Merchant's Mile** | ~1,200 | Trade settlement. Caravanserais, warehouses, factors' offices. Everything is taxed. |

**Key Locations in Accord's Rest:**
- **The High Citadel** — Helmite fortress-temple. Commander-General Aldric Vorn commands. Dungeons seven levels deep. The Eye of Helm stares from the highest tower.
- **The Hall of Scales** — Tyrran courthouse complex. High Magistrate Seraphina Cole presides. Trials public. Sentencing swift. Executioner's block sees regular use.
- **The Black Fist Garrison** — Banite compound, eastern edge. Inquisitor Malrek Thorne arrived six months ago. Includes the **Black Threshold**: cells, interrogation chambers, a chapel where pain is prayer. Prisoners of particular interest are taken here. Few return.
- **The Beneath** — Undercity. Officially sewers. Actually Maskarran smuggling network, safe houses, spy ring. The Whisper commands. The Accord has tried to clear it three times. It fills back up.
- **The Memorial of Order** — Public square. Statues of Helm, Tyr, Bane. Eternal flame "in memory of those lost to chaos." Locals avoid lingering.
- **The Twilight Market** — Semi-legal evening market. Licensed goods above board, everything else below. Raided occasionally. Always reforms.

### The Four Vales
*Fertile lowlands, agricultural heartland, and broken traditions.*

Once four vales, now three. The fourth is a wound.

| Settlement | Pop. | Description |
|------------|------|-------------|
| **Summervale** | ~5,000 | Murdock's origin. Families torn apart during the Pacification—children taken, parents arrested, records sealed. Fields productive; people hollow. |
| **Autumnvale** | ~6,000 | Wolfric's origin. Ilmatari church still operates. Brother Aldous tends the faithful. Twelve-sibling families, strong community. Accord watches closely. |
| **Harvestholm** | ~1,800 | Market town. Grain silos, livestock pens, Tyrran factors. Population doubles at harvest. |
| **Millbrook** | ~700 | Quiet village. Apple orchards, cider. Too small to garrison. |
| **Shepherd's Cross** | ~400 | Crossroads village for drovers. Helmite waystation, easy posting. |
| **Wintervale** | — | *"Spring and Summer, Autumn's gold / Winter's tale is never told."* |

**The Wintervale Question:** Wintervale refused the Accord's oaths. A Lliiran shrine stood at its heart, tended by a priestess named Elara Dawnlight. Banite forces were deployed. The shrine burned. The priestess vanished. Survivors scattered. Maps made after show empty hills. The land today is empty grassland—no wildflowers, no trees, no wildlife. Some claim to see festival lights on winter nights.

### The Joybright Coast
*Where joy died. Haunted shores and forbidden waters.*

# Part Four: The Enforcement Machine

## How Control Works Day-to-Day

### Helmites — The Visible Hand
*"We protect. That's what this is."*

Patrol and presence. Checkpoints. Harbor inspections. Contraband confiscation (scarlet ribbons, unauthorized holy symbols, the Book of Exultation, certain songs—the list grows regularly—dancing masks, any depiction of Lliira). Arrest and detention. Standing watch.

They are the most common Accord face. Most believe they're helping. The cognitive dissonance between "guardian" and "enforcer" creates fractures. Signs of doubt: hesitation before orders, "failing to notice" minor infractions, asking *why* instead of just *how*.

### Tyrrans — The Legal Machine
*"The law is clear."*

Courts process dissent through proper channels. Arrest (Helmite) → Trial (Tyrran) → Sentence (Tyrran). The process is meticulous. Evidence weighed, witnesses heard, accused may speak. Then convicted, because the laws criminalize any deviation from approved worship by definition.

**Sentencing:**
- Unlicensed worship → Fine, confiscation, mandatory approved services
- Forbidden materials → Months to years imprisonment, property seizure
- Active dissent → Extended imprisonment, forced labor, family separation
- Sedition → Public trial, execution or life imprisonment
- Heresy → Death

### Banites — The Iron Fist
*"We ask questions. We don't ask twice."*

Inquisition, terror, special operations. Called in when dissent persists. Their authority supersedes local Helmite and Tyrran leadership. In Ghandara: small but growing. The Black Threshold in Accord's Rest is where prisoners of particular interest go. Few return.

### The Shadow Apparatus

**Sharrans** — Informants embedded in communities. "Grief counselors" who report dissent. Cultivate despair to weaken resistance.

**Loviatans** — Specialist interrogators for prisoners who won't break. They take their time.

**Cyricans** — Disinformation, entrapment, agents provocateur. Forge evidence. Spread false rumors. The **Compliance Ministry** operates with heavy Cyrican influence—this is the branch that sent Nessandrle.

### Enforcement Gradient

| Distance from Capital | Presence | Feel |
|----------------------|----------|------|
| Outer islands / frontier | Minimal Helmite outpost | Relative freedom |
| Minor settlements | Small garrison, visiting Justicar | Light occupation |
| Mid-tier ports (Kell's Landing) | Chapterhouse, courthouse, occasional Banite | Occupation with civil face |
| Major cities | All three in force | Bureaucratic oppression |
| Accord's Rest | Everything, overlapping | Totalitarian theocracy |

# Part Six: Campaign Secrets & BBEG Groundwork

*DM eyes only.*

## The Truth About Wintervale

Wintervale didn't just resist. It *succeeded*, briefly. Elara Dawnlight channeled enough divine power to hold off the Banite assault for three days. Something happened during that stand that the Accord has worked to erase.

**Elara didn't die.** She transcended, or was taken, or became something else. The land isn't empty—it's *waiting*. The festival lights on winter nights are real: echoes of a celebration that never ended, playing in a place adjacent to reality. Elara Dawnlight is still celebrating somewhere just out of reach.

**Campaign use:** Wintervale is a beacon. If the Lastbloom is planted there, or if enough joy is kindled in that place, the echo might *bleed through*. Wintervale could return. Or something could come *out*.

## What's at Death's Edge

Six months ago, miners struck a chamber. Ancient, pre-human, sealed with magic predating the gods' current forms. The Accord sent scholars, priests, and finally Inquisitor Thorne. The chamber contains a fragment of something *old*. Old enough to remember when gods could die—because it killed some of them, long ago.

**Campaign use:** This is why Thorne is really in Ghandara. Not for dissenters—for a *weapon*. Bane wants the ability to kill gods permanently, without the messy coalition effort that Lliira's execution required. If he gets it, no dissenter god is safe.

## The Joybright Anomaly

Joybright isn't just haunted—it's *protected*. Lliira's death released a final gift into the city. The buildings don't decay because joy preserves them. The laughter at night is real—echoes of ten thousand festivals.

And something is waking up. Not Lliira—she's truly dead. But divinity leaves marks. Enough worship in one place for enough centuries creates *possibilities*. The Accord's garrison isn't there to keep people out. It's there to keep something in.

**Campaign use:** If the Lastbloom reaches Joybright, if the party can kindle genuine joy in the city, the something might fully awaken. A new entity? A fragment of Lliira reborn? A spirit of joy itself, without a god's form but with a god's power? This is the campaign's endgame territory.

## Bane's Actual Plan (BBEG Framework)

The Accord was always Bane's tool. Helm and Tyr are *useful*—they provide legitimacy and manpower—but they also *limit* him. Bane's long game:

1. **The Death's Edge weapon** — A means to kill gods without coalition. Once Bane has this, he doesn't need the Accord's consensus to eliminate threats.
2. **Discredit Helm** — Bane is maneuvering to put Helmites in positions where they must commit atrocities that fracture the order from within. The more Helmites doubt, the weaker Helm becomes, the more Bane can fill the gap.
3. **Subvert Tyr** — Bane's agents within the Tyrran courts are pushing legal precedents that expand Banite authority. Each "emergency measure" becomes permanent. Each "temporary" power becomes entrenched.
4. **Eliminate dissenters permanently** — Lliira was the test case. Tymora, Mask, Selûne are next. Once the opposition is gone, the Accord becomes Bane's personal theocracy.
5. **The endgame** — A world where Bane is not one god among many but the *only* god that matters. Not through worship—through *dependency*. Mortals who cannot conceive of existence without his authority.

**The ticking clock:** Thorne's investigation at Death's Edge. If the party doesn't act, Bane gets his weapon. If Bane gets his weapon, the campaign shifts from resistance to survival.

## Ilmater's Counter-Plan

Ilmater has been watching. Suffering. Enduring. For forty years, he's accepted punishment rather than enforce the Accord. The other gods think he's broken.

He's not broken. He's been *paying attention*.

Ilmater knows about the Lastbloom. He may have helped create it—or at least, he didn't prevent it. He knows about Wintervale. He knows about Death's Edge. He knows what Bane is planning.

His faithful—the Unbroken—are positioned across Ghandara. Brother Aldous in Autumnvale. The Broken Tower keeper in Kell's Landing. Others, unnamed, in every vale and village where suffering exists and someone chooses to *bear it for others*.

When the time comes—when Wolfric and Murdock prove themselves, when the Lastbloom awakens, when the party has earned it—Ilmater will act. Not with force. Not with violence. But with the thing he's been gathering for forty years of endurance: *moral authority*. The weight of a god who has suffered everything the Accord has inflicted and *refused to break*.

What that looks like in play is up to you. But the seeds are planted.
