# The Fog and the Thorns

_Session Four_

---

_The dead don't stay quiet on ships like this. Neither do the living._

## A Son's Grief

The blood was still drying on the deck when **DaVonian Brightwell** came up from below.

He'd heard the fighting. He'd heard the silence that followed. He'd heard his mother's voice go from commands to nothing at all. And now he stood at the top of the stairs and saw his father's body crumpled on the planks, and the man who'd killed him standing over it.

Something broke in the boy. Not loudly, not with screaming or wailing, though those would come later. It broke the way a branch breaks under too much snow. Quietly, completely, all at once.

DaVonian drew a blade and lunged at **Karsa**. 

There was no technique. No thought. Just a fifteen-year-old boy with a knife and a world that had taken everything from him in the span of a single morning. Murdock caught him, wrapped arms around the boy and held him while DaVonian thrashed and sobbed and tried to cut something, anything, because if he couldn't hurt the people who'd destroyed his family then at least he could hurt _someone_.

Murdock held on. The boy's rage spent itself against the monk's steady grip like a wave against stone. Eventually the thrashing stopped. The sobs didn't.

---

## The Oath on the Water

While grief consumed the lower deck, **Wolfric** stood at the bow.

The paladin of Ilmater looked out over the Mirthwater, two ships drifting side by side, one emptied by violence, the other hollowed by it. Behind him, refugees huddled and whispered and tried to understand what kind of people they'd trusted their lives to.

Wolfric didn't explain. He didn't apologize. He _prayed_.

And then he spoke.

Not to the gods. To the people on the ship. To the living. His voice carried across the deck. He spoke of endurance. Of bearing what cannot be borne. Of standing up when the world says to kneel. A man of faith, scarred and stubborn, who would carry their suffering because that was what Ilmater asked of him. The refugees listened.

The Book of Exultation was still with **Karsa**. That detail sat in the silence like a blade on a table.

---

## A Course Unchanged

Things calmed, eventually. The screaming stopped and the tears ran dry and what was left was the hard, cold question that had been waiting since the first sail was raised: _where do we go?_

Discussion moved through the party like wind through rigging. Arguments were made, abandoned, remade. But in the end, nothing had changed. The destination that Marren had chosen, that the refugees had been promised, still waited on the horizon.

**Joybright.**

The ship would hold its course. Straight ahead. Into whatever was left of a holy city that had died the same day as its goddess.

Evening settled over the water like a blanket pulled up over a wound. The ship sailed on.

---

## Morning Light

Dawn found the ship transformed.

Below deck, something impossible was happening. The refugees, those same huddled, grieving, terrified souls who had watched a prophet die and a killer walk free, were _singing_.

Quietly at first. One voice, then two, then a dozen. Old hymns. Lliiran melodies that had been forbidden for twenty-three years. Songs their parents taught them in whispers, songs they'd never been allowed to sing aloud. And they were singing them now, below the deck of a blood-stained ship, because something in the night had shifted.

**Tessa** had the Book again.

She stood among her followers with the Book of Exultation open in her lap, and she was reading. The maternal warmth was back, the steady voice, the gentle authority, the mask so practiced it might as well have been her real face. Whatever had broken in her when Marren died had been sealed over, paved smooth, rebuilt into something functional if not whole.

She called **Wolfric** to her and asked him to read as well. He did. His voice was nothing like Marren's, no warmth of summer festivals, no scent of honey wine. But the words responded to him anyway. The Book didn't care about polish. It cared about conviction.

Then **Karsa** came forward.

---

## The Book Changes Hands

Karsa wanted the Book. Whether out of obligation, possessiveness, or something darker, he confronted Tessa and demanded it. The exchange was tense but brief. She had no choice. Karsa was not a man you refused.

But Karsa didn't keep it long.

**Murdock** moved. Fast, fluid, the way he'd moved when he stole Nessandrle's lute in the Golden Chain. He grabbed the Book from Karsa's hands and _ran_.

The deck erupted. Refugees surged. Some tried to stop Murdock. The Book was _theirs_, their prophet's legacy, the last holy thing in a world that had murdered holiness. Others froze, unsure whose side they were on, unsure if _sides_ was even the right word for what was happening.

The party fractured. Wolfric, Paul, Murdock — none of them knew where the others stood. Allegiances that had seemed solid an hour ago crumbled like wet sand.

And then **Tessa** went quiet.

Not her usual calculated silence. Not the pause before a sermon or the breath before a command. She simply... stopped speaking. Stopped moving. Her eyes went somewhere else.

The fog came with her silence.

---

## The Fog

It rolled in from being cast from Lil Moon. The book and its followers ran up the higher deck.s

**DaVonian** emerged from the fog swinging wildly.

Not the grief-stricken boy who had sobbed in Murdock's arms. Something else. Something hollowed out and filled back up with rage that didn't belong to a fifteen-year-old. He came from the stern like a storm given legs, blade flashing, cutting at anything that moved.

**Karsa** tried to run. The blade found him anyway — a slash across the back, drawing blood from a man twice the boy's size. Karsa staggered. The fog pressed in.

Up top, **Wolfric**, **Murdock**, and **Paul** climbed the mast. They had the Book. They had distance. They had each other. Below them, the fog churned like a living thing, and from within it came the sounds of steel and screaming.

They talked. Up there in the rigging, above the madness, clinging to ropes and each other, they tried to make sense of what was happening. The Book sat between them — warm, pulsing, oblivious to the chaos below. A love letter from a dead goddess, caught between men who didn't know what to do with it.

Below, the fog consumed everything.

**Karsa** was struck again. DaVonian's blade bit deep — too deep for a teenager, too precise for blind rage. Whatever was driving the boy was not grief alone.

Then the fog dropped.

---

## The Unmasking

The scene that greeted them was a nightmare painted in morning light.

**Tessa Brightwell** stood at the center of the deck, and she was _changed_.

The maternal warmth was gone. The practiced smile, the gentle voice, the careful performance of faith she'd maintained for ten years — all of it stripped away like a mask torn free by wind. What stood in its place was something older, rawer, and infinitely more dangerous. A woman who had built her entire identity on stolen conviction and watched it crumble, and in the crumbling had found something real and terrible.

Her followers surrounded her. Not the frightened refugees who had been singing hymns an hour ago — _followers_. Eyes glazed, bodies swaying, mouths moving in unison with words no one could hear. They had crossed some threshold in the fog, pulled by whatever power Tessa had been drawing from without knowing or caring.

Then the refugees began to die.

Not at Tessa's hand. At their own. Several turned their blades inward — quick, deliberate, final. They fell without screaming. The rest shuffled toward Tessa like sleepwalkers drawn to a fire, and it was impossible to tell if they were joining her or surrendering to her.

**Karsa** killed DaVonian.

The words are simple. The act was not. Whatever had possessed the boy, whatever supernatural fury had been driving his blade, it ended when Karsa's greatsword came down. DaVonian Brightwell, fifteen years old, son of a false prophet and a dead priest, fell on the deck his father had fallen on hours before.

Karsa didn't stop moving. He pushed toward Tessa, toward the knot of entranced followers that surrounded her.

Then the thorns came.

---

## Wall of Thorns

**Lil Moon** acted.

The druid had been watching, waiting, feeling the wrongness in the air the way he felt the Lastbloom pulse against his chest. He spoke to the wild, and the wild answered. A wall of thorns erupted from the deck — massive, twisting, impossibly alive — and wrapped itself around Tessa and her followers like a fist closing.

The thorns did not discriminate. The followers died instantly — crushed, pierced, consumed by vegetation that grew with supernatural hunger. Only Tessa survived within the cage, bleeding, screaming, still radiating that terrible changed energy.

**Wolfric** arrived too late to stop the killing. But not too late to save.

The paladin dropped from the mast and ran to DaVonian's broken body. The boy was dying — breath rattling, blood pooling, the light behind his eyes flickering like a candle in wind. Wolfric knelt beside him and reached for something deeper than medicine.

Ilmater's boon.

The gift the Crying God had granted to his faithful paladin — the ability to take another's suffering as his own. Wolfric laid hands on DaVonian and _pulled_. The boy's dying condition flowed into the paladin like poison into a wound. DaVonian gasped. Color returned to his face. His eyes opened.

Wolfric collapsed.

For one terrible moment, the paladin of Ilmater lay on the deck bearing the weight of a death that wasn't his. His heart slowed. His vision darkened.

Then he rolled a natural twenty.

Wolfric's eyes snapped open. He surged upright, alive, burning, Ilmater's light blazing through him like dawn through stained glass. The Crying God didn't just _allow_ him to survive — he was _answered_. Immediately, completely, as if endurance itself had been weighed and found worthy.

"Finish it," Wolfric told DaVonian, looking toward Karsa. "End this."

---

## Lightning and Loss

DaVonian and Wolfric ran together toward Karsa, the boy's blade drawn, purpose replacing the hollow rage that had consumed him in the fog. This time it was _him_ — not possessed, not driven, just a son who had watched everyone he loved destroyed and decided to make it mean something.

They struck Karsa.

The lightning hit them both.

**Water Walt's** shield — a crackling barrier of electricity the highland healer had placed on Karsa for protection — detonated on contact. The energy tore through DaVonian and Wolfric simultaneously. The boy who had just been saved from death met it again in a flash of white light. The paladin who had given everything to save him watched it undone in an instant.

DaVonian Brightwell died. Again. This time, for good.

Wolfric went down beside him. The boon spent. The miracle wasted. Two bodies on a deck that had collected too many.

---

## The Thorns Close

Tessa was still alive inside Lil Moon's cage. Still fighting. Still focused on Karsa with a hatred that had transcended anything human — the cold, desperate fury of a woman watching the last pillars of her constructed world collapse around her.

Lil Moon spoke to the plants.

Not a command. A conversation. The druid's voice was quiet, patient, the same tone he used with growing things in forests far from ships and blood. _Move_, he told them. _Close._

The thorns listened.

They shifted inward. Slowly. Not the violent crush that had killed the followers but something worse — deliberate, inevitable, tightening like a noose woven from green wood and sharp edges. Tessa fought against them. Her magic flared. Her voice rose in something that might have been a prayer or a curse or both.

It wasn't enough.

The thorns consumed her. Inch by inch, spine by spine, until the woman who had stolen a prophet's mantle and worn it for ten years was pressed and pierced and finally, mercifully, still.

In her last moments, Tessa Brightwell was not looking at her killers. She was not looking at the Book or the sky or the sea.

She was looking at her son's body.

Whatever mask she'd worn, whatever lie she'd told, whatever power she'd borrowed or stolen or corrupted — in the end, she was a mother watching her child die. And that, at least, had never been a performance.

---

## Silence

The thorns withered. The fog was gone. The morning sun fell on a deck painted in blood and green and the terrible quiet that comes after everything that can break has broken.

Thirteen adults. Six children. That was what remained of thirty Lliiran refugees who had sailed from Chult seeking a dead city and a dead goddess and something worth believing in.

**Wolfric** lay unconscious on the deck. Alive, but spent — Ilmater's boon exhausted, his body a map of wounds both his and borrowed.

**Paul** had angered **Karsa**. The details were almost irrelevant against the scale of what had happened, but the tension was real and sharp and pointed, and Karsa was not a man who forgot offenses.

The Book of Exultation lay somewhere on the ship. Warm. Patient. Waiting for someone to open it and remember what joy used to feel like.

Somewhere ahead, across water that had earned its name from celebrations that would never come again, Joybright waited. A holy city killed by the same Accord that had driven these people to sea. A ruin that soldiers patrolled and still heard laughter in at night.

The ship sailed on. Nineteen survivors. Two bodies. One destination.

No one sang.

---

_The fog came from within. The thorns grew from below. And when the morning cleared, the ship carried less than half of what it started with._

_Joybright waits. It has been waiting for twenty-three years. It can wait a little longer._

_The question is whether anyone on this ship can._
