# The Weeping Shore

_Session Five_

---

_The dead don't argue about where to go next. That's the only advantage they have over the living._

## Quiet Waters

The ship drifted on. Behind them: blood, thorns, and bodies. Ahead: a coastline none of them had ever seen.

Wolfric lay on the deck, pale as sailcloth, breathing but absent. Whatever Ilmater's boon had given, the price had been paid in full. He didn't stir when the children cried. Didn't stir when the adults stopped crying. Didn't stir when the silence became worse than both.

The party stood on deck and didn't look at each other.

There had been words, after. Sharp ones, quiet ones, the kind that cut deeper for being said calmly. But the sea doesn't care about grudges. It asks only one question: _where are you going?_

They answered it. Joybright. Still Joybright. Despite everything. Maybe because of everything. They navigated the Mirthwater carefully, charting around the patrol routes they'd pulled from dead men's maps, slipping through waters the Accord thought it controlled.

They made it. Barely.

But the Forbidden Zone was another matter. Even from a distance, the sea near Joybright felt _wrong_. The party looked at that stillness and made the practical choice for once.

They turned south. To Greyshore.

## The Forgotten Village

Greyshore announced itself the way poor places do: slowly, and without enthusiasm. A handful of buildings hunched along the waterline. Fishing boats pulled up on near grey rock. Nets drying on racks that hadn't been mended in too long. The smell of salt and seaweed and the particular kind of despair that comes from hauling empty nets season after season.

_The sea itself is grieving,_ the fishermen said. Had been since the execution. The catch had never recovered.

The party noticed the Helmite immediately. He was sitting on a crate near the dock, in armor that had seen better years, watching their ship approach with all the urgency of a man watching paint dry.

He did nothing.

Didn't stand. Didn't call out. Didn't reach for a weapon or a signal horn or a sending stone. Just sat there, a monument to the particular kind of surrender that happens when a soldier realizes no one is coming to relieve him and no one cares if he does his job.

**Private Aldren Marsh.** was walking the docks drunks. A man who wished to not be bothered. The party bothered him anyway.

Aldren had the watery eyes and loose posture of a man who'd been drinking since before lunch and intended to continue well past supper. He offered no resistance to being collected, spouted noisy information about this or that, and no explanation for why he was here instead of at the garrison. He just came along, dragged toward the only tavern in town like a dog too tired to pull against the leash.

## The Saltstone Inn

**Eadra Finn** ran the Saltstone Inn the way a captain runs a ship in bad weather: with grim competence and no patience for passengers who couldn't pull their weight.

She was a broad woman, hard-handed, with eyes that catalogued every newcomer in the time it took them to cross her threshold. She'd been in Greyshore long enough to remember when the nets came up full and the air smelled like salt instead of sorrow. Long enough to have opinions about everything and everyone, and no reason to keep them to herself.

She saw Aldren and her expression curdled.

"Not him," she said. "He's run up a tab that'd buy a fishing boat. Two fishing boats. And he hasn't paid a copper of it."

The objection was real, but so was the concession beneath it. She'd let him drink on credit for months, maybe longer. Not because she was generous, Eadra Finn did not strike anyone as generous, but because of his father. Whatever debt of respect or history or obligation connected Private Aldren Marsh to this innkeeper, it had been stretched to breaking.

Karsa solved the problem the way Karsa solved most problems: directly. He paid the tab. All of it. Then bought rounds for the table, for the bar, for anyone who looked thirsty. Gold meant nothing to a man who carried the weight Karsa carried. Coin was easy. Everything else was impossible.

The ale flowed. The Saltstone Inn, for the first time in what felt like a very long while, sounded almost like a tavern.

## The Drinking Contest

It started, as these things do, with someone who should've known better.

A paladin at the bar, armored, confident, the kind of man who announces himself by the space he takes up, watched Karsa drink and saw an opportunity. Or a challenge. Or just an excuse.

"A contest," he said. "Drink for drink. Winner takes a keg of Eagles Rest highland ale. Finest brew north of the Geast Range."

Whether the keg was real or the claim was true didn't matter. What mattered was that Karsa said yes.

Eight rounds. A bottle of cheap wine per round. The paladin was no amateur, he had the build and the constitution of a man who'd trained his liver as seriously as his sword arm. But Karsa was something else. Karsa drank the way he fought: methodically, without pleasure, as if the act itself was just another thing to be endured and conquered.

Round after round, the bottles emptied. The paladin's face reddened. His boasts got louder, then quieter, then stopped entirely. By the seventh round his eyes had gone glassy. By the eighth he was swaying like a mast in a storm.

Karsa set down his last empty bottle. The paladin pitched forward and hit the floor face-first with a sound like a sack of grain dropped from a wagon.

Karsa collected the keg. He didn't smile.

## The Man in the Corner

While Karsa drank his way through the paladin's pride, Paul found something more interesting.

A figure sat alone in the corner of the bar, nursing a drink with the careful attention of someone making it last. Thin. Pale. The kind of face that blended into walls and slipped out of memories. He introduced himself as **Lucien**, though the name sat on him loosely, like borrowed clothes.

Paul sat down. They talked.

It was the kind of conversation that starts with nothing and builds toward something without either party admitting it. Books. Power. The weakness of people who confused authority with strength. The difference between believing in something and _using_ belief. Words circled each other like fighters testing range.

The others drifted over. The conversation widened. Lucien spoke carefully, measuring each word the way a man measures rope before cutting, never too much, never wasteful.

They opened the Eagles Rest ale. Karsa's prize, shared among strangers who were becoming something else. The highland brew was amber and warm and tasted of heather and high places, and it did something to each of them. Paul's eyes went distant, dreaming of something that might have been memory or might have been hope. Even Karsa's jaw unclenched, just for a moment, just enough to suggest that somewhere beneath the stone there was still something that could feel. Lucian and Water Walt laughed and laughed.

The Lastbloom, hidden among Lil Moon's things, pulsed faintly.

## Unwelcome Company

The warmth didn't last. It never does.

Two figures approached the table. The first was small, human, nervous, the kind of man who stands slightly behind larger people for a reason. The second was an orc, broad as a doorframe and twice as immovable, who introduced himself as **Sir Dalton** with the particular emphasis of someone who wanted the title noticed.

Sir Dalton wanted ale. The fine ale. In exchange, he offered information. The sort of vague, self-important information that goes nowhere and means nothing, a tale that stretched and wandered and circled back on itself without ever arriving at a point. Something about weapons to kill a god, patrols, mines... nonsense.

The party heard none of it. Whatever patience they'd carried into Greyshore had been spent on worse men than this, and they had nothing left for a stranger whose currency was hot air.

Sir Dalton pressed. The party refused. The conversation curdled.

It was Lucien who ended it. The pale man had stepped away from the table, slipped away, really, the way shadows slip when the light shifts, and from outside the tavern came the unmistakable sound of a Helmite presence. Boots on stone. The clink of armor. A voice carrying authority.

It was a lie. A perfect, practiced lie. Lucien had conjured a threat from nothing, and it worked exactly as intended.

Sir Dalton's composure shattered. The orc and his small companion bolted for the back, convinced they were being watched, convinced the Accord had eyes on them. They vanished into the Greyshore night like they'd never been there at all.

Eadra stormed outside, furious. She found nothing, no Helmites, no patrol, just empty streets and the sound of the sea. But fury needs a target, and she found one at the guard shack.

She descended on the garrison like a squall on a fishing boat. Demanded to know why her patrons were being harassed. The guards, three tired men who wanted nothing more than to finish their shift, denied everything. She dragged them back to the tavern anyway, guards in tow like chastened children, and turned her attention to the newcomers at the table.

"Someone's paying for this disruption," a guard announced. "Justice or coin."

Lucien, with the quiet resignation of a man used to paying for his cleverness, produced his purse. Gold changed hands. The guards left. Eadra returned to her bar, mollified but not satisfied.

The evening settled, but something about it felt unfinished. Like a knot tied too loosely.

## The Plan

The party gathered one last time around the table, voices low, the tavern emptying around them.

Joybright. Still Joybright. Always Joybright.

But not by sea. The ship had carried them this far, and it had cost them more than distance. The Forbidden Zone was no place for a vessel full of refugees and children. The water route was watched, patrolled, and haunted by things worse than Helmites.

Lucien knew another way. A road along the coast, skirting the shore, leading south toward the ruined city. Overland. On foot. No sails to spot, no hull to board, no sending stones to track.

The party agreed. They collected their gear, said what needed saying to the refugees still aboard the ship, and walked away from the dock.

Behind them, the ship began to move. The refugees — 7 adults, six children, the remnants of thirty souls who'd sailed from Chult chasing a dead goddess's promise, were sailing on without them. Where, exactly, was their business now. The party had brought them this far. The rest of the journey belonged to someone else.

The children watched from the rail as the shore fell away.

## The Weeping Stones

They marched north along the coast, boots on wet stone, the sea to their right and the wild country to their left. Lucien set the pace, quick, confident, a man who knew the ground beneath his feet.

Good time. Better than expected. The road, such as it was, followed the shoreline's curve, rising and falling with the land. Salt air. Grey sky. The call of gulls that didn't know the world had ended.

And then they came upon the wall.

It rose from the coastline like a wound — raw stone, pale and striated, layered with deposits of white salt that caught the fading light and _ran_. Not water. Not quite. But the resemblance was unmistakable. The salt traced lines down the rock face in long, unbroken streaks, converging and diverging, pooling in the hollows and threading through the cracks.

The Weeping Stones.

They looked exactly like tears.

The party stopped. Even Karsa stopped Though the names etched on the wall only seemed like the names of the weak to him.

_The Accord knows people visit,_ the lore said. _They haven't been able to stop them without posting guards on every cliff._

They made camp. A small cave offered shelter from the wind, though not much else. The walls were close and the air was damp and something bit at exposed skin - insects, probably, the ordinary kind that don't care about divine tragedy or political oppression.

But there was something else in that cave. Not a sound. Not a presence. Just a feeling, sitting in the chest like a held breath. An ominousness that had no source and no name.

Lil Moon's pack glowed faintly. The Lastbloom, stirring in its sleep.

They were close now. Joybright was to the east, and whatever waited there,  laughter in empty streets, buildings untouched by time, the ghost of a goddess or the echo of a celebration that never ended,  it was calling.

Tomorrow, they walk.

---

_The stones weep because someone has to._

_The sea grieves because the catch never came back._

_And somewhere south of here, in a city that should be ruins but isn't, lanterns burn in windows where no one lives, and the sound of dancing carries on a wind that doesn't blow._

_Joybright waits. It's always been waiting._

_They're almost there._
