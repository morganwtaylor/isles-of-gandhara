# The Isles of Ghandara — Act One

## "Can Joy Survive?"

_Sessions 1–7_

---

# The Day Joy Died

The sky broke on a Tuesday morning.

In Kell's Landing, on the southern coast of Ghandara, people were doing what people do. Wolfric was buying supplies at the market. Lil Moon was hauling cargo on the docks. Murdock was keeping his head down and his fists ready. Thirty Lliiran refugees on a battered ship were praying for safe passage to a holy city called Joybright.

Then the light changed.

The sun didn't dim — the quality shifted. Shadows fell wrong. Sound dropped out for a single heartbeat. And above the western horizon, the sky bloomed scarlet and gold, beautiful and terrible, and in that impossible light, shapes moved: a figure in golden armor weeping, a figure in gray steel with arms wrapped around something that shone, and a figure in black iron with his hand raised in judgment.

In the gray figure's grip — a woman made of light and laughter, red hair like festival ribbons, dressed in scarlet that flowed like water. She reached toward the world below.

The sword fell. She didn't scream.

She laughed. One last sound — bright, defiant, heartbroken. Then the scarlet light detonated, and every happy memory anyone ever had flashed through their minds simultaneously.

Then it was gone.

Lliira, Goddess of Joy, was dead. Bane pronounced sentence. Helm held her still. Tyr swung the blade. The message was clear: even gods kneel or die.

The Helmites came within the hour. Ship to ship along the docks, dragging away anyone bound for Joybright. Families. Children. An old man humming a forbidden hymn. When the patrol reached Tessa Brightwell's ship, our party stood their ground.

Vigilant Corus Thane — the same petty thief who had stolen Wolfric's purse that morning — killed three refugees on the deck before the party cut him down. His own soldiers watched and did not intervene. None of them had liked him much.

That night, Murdock knelt in the underground sanctuary of the Broken Tower and swore the Oath of Ilmater over the body of a sick child. The cord was tied. The vows were spoken. A man who had never followed gods became a bearer of light in dark places.

Thirty lives saved. One Helmite dead. The Accord would not forget.

---

# The Road South

Paul appeared at the docks the next morning — slipping through the city like smoke, joining the ship without ceremony. Whatever brought him to Kell's Landing, his path led to these people.

Tessa Brightwell talked them past the harbor inspection with the practiced confidence of a woman who had been performing authority for a decade. Papers produced, questions answered, suspicions smoothed. The ship sailed free.

Six days at sea. Wolfric and Murdock prayed to Ilmater and felt something stir in response. The refugees sang quietly below deck. Tessa read from the Book of Exultation — an ancient scarlet volume in soft leather that smelled of summer festivals and honey wine. Her voice was polished, warm, practiced. A performance.

On the seventh day, they made port at Thornhaven.

---

# The Golden Chain

Thornhaven was a working town — shipwrights and timber and the smell of sawdust and brine. Seven thousand souls under moderate Accord control. The party went to a tavern called the Golden Chain and found trouble waiting.

Marren Brightwell was there. Tessa's husband — arrested ten years ago for possessing the Book of Exultation. Somehow free. Traveling with a towering warrior named Karsa, a man of few words and terrifying competence. Marren reunited with Tessa at the docks. Whatever joy should have been in that reunion was complicated by a decade of questions neither could answer.

At the tavern, things went sideways fast. An aasimar named Seraph sat in the corner and attempted to judge Wolfric — some divine authority older than the Accord. A bard called Nessandrle began playing, and slowly, sweetly, the music wrapped around the party's minds like warm hands. Enchantment magic. Compliance Ministry.

Murdock broke the spell by stealing the lute and running.

The party scattered through Thornhaven's streets. Karsa stayed behind to fight Nessandrle in a back room while Tonyult — proprietor of the Golden Chain, information broker, neutral party — sat and watched with professional interest. Both Karsa and the bard went down.

Tonyult made a business decision. He stabilized both. And he sent Karsa a message: a debt owed, to be collected later. Three days to kill Marren Brightwell.

---

# Blood on the Mirthwater

They sailed the next morning with Marren and Karsa aboard. A Helmite cutter intercepted them on the Mirthwater — the inland sea that separated the world they knew from the world they were sailing toward.

The battle was brief and brutal. The party took the cutter. Its crew died. And in the chaos, Marren Brightwell performed the one miracle his wife never could — a pillar of divine light that turned the tide.

Moments later, Karsa killed him. The contract fulfilled. Three days to kill Marren, done in one.

The Book of Exultation fell from dead hands. It still smelled of summer festivals. It still radiated warmth. It waited for someone to pick it up.

Tessa Brightwell watched her husband die and went silent.

---

# The Ship That Broke

Two ships drifted north on the Mirthwater. One empty. One carrying thirty broken people, five adventurers, one highland healer, and one killer.

DaVonian Brightwell — Tessa's fifteen-year-old son — came below deck and found his father's body. He broke. He lashed out at Murdock, who held him. The party talked him down. Things seemed to calm.

They didn't.

The morning brought false hope. Refugees singing below deck. Tessa with the Book again, reading aloud, her voice steady and warm and wrong. A fog rolled in — thick, unnatural, tasting of bile. DaVonian emerged from below swinging wildly, his eyes unfocused, his movements not entirely his own.

Tessa had changed. Whatever faith she'd been performing for a decade curdled into something real and terrible. The refugees around her went still, then shuffled toward her, blank-eyed, entranced. Several killed themselves. The rest pressed closer, drawn by a gravity that had nothing to do with devotion.

Lil Moon acted. A wall of thorns erupted from the deck and wrapped around Tessa and her followers like a closing fist. The thorns did not discriminate. Her followers died. Tessa survived inside the cage, bleeding, screaming, still pulling at the world around her.

Wolfric arrived too late to stop the killing. Not too late to save. He dropped beside DaVonian's broken body and reached for Ilmater's boon — the gift the Crying God had granted his faithful paladin. The ability to take another's dying. Wolfric pulled the death from the boy and took it into himself.

He rolled a natural twenty. Ilmater answered. Wolfric surged upright, blazing, and told DaVonian to finish Karsa. They ran together.

They struck Karsa. Water Walt's lightning shield detonated on contact. DaVonian and Wolfric went down together — the boy killed for the second and final time, the paladin felled by the mercy of an ally's protection spell.

In the thorns, Tessa died. Lil Moon spoke to the plants and the cage closed. In her last moments, she wasn't looking at her killers or the Book or the sky. She was looking at her son's body.

The ship went silent. Thirteen adults. Six children. That was what remained.

---

# Greyshore

They sailed north with a deck they couldn't clean and grief they couldn't carry. The Lastbloom — the scarlet flower that had found Lil Moon in the chaos — pulsed gently in his hands, pulling toward something ahead. The Book of Exultation lay warm and patient, waiting for someone to read it honestly.

Paul picked it up. New passages appeared for him that had been invisible for a decade. The Book was choosing.

They put in at Greyshore — a fishing village of eleven hundred souls on the coast north of Joybright. Poor catch since the execution. The fishermen said the sea itself was grieving. The party left the refugees here under Water Walt's care and continued south on foot.

In Greyshore, they found Pvt. Aldren Marsh — a Weeping Watch soldier who had deserted his post, raving about what he'd seen in Joybright's Sunken Temple. Golden light. Laughter. Something alive in a city that should be dead. His testimony gave them intel: garrison layout, patrol schedules, and a back way in past the wards.

The party walked toward Joybright. Behind them, nineteen survivors and a highland healer. Ahead, a murdered city and whatever was waiting inside it.

---

# The Weeping Stones

They stopped at the Weeping Stones — coastal cliffs where salt deposits created the appearance of tears streaming down the rock face. A secret pilgrim site. In a small dark alcove, Paul sat down with the Book of Exultation and began to read aloud.

The attunement took an hour. During that hour, Paul experienced a thousand celebrations he'd never attended — festivals in cities he'd never visited, songs in languages he didn't speak, the feeling of dancing with strangers and meaning it. The Book flooded him with centuries of joy compressed into sixty minutes.

The alcove responded. The salt tears on the walls ran fresh — not brine, warm water. The stones wept with joy for the first time since Lliira died. Golden light traced old carvings on the walls: pilgrim prayers scratched into the rock over centuries, hundreds of them, a record of faith older than anyone knew.

And one carving that wasn't a prayer. A map — a hidden pilgrim route from the Weeping Stones directly into Joybright, bypassing the garrison's sight lines. The Book lit it up because Paul was the first genuine seeker to attune in this place.

The Lastbloom bloomed wider in Lil Moon's hands. Both artifacts singing to each other. The road ahead was clear.

---
# Joybright

The holy city that was.

They entered through the hidden pilgrim path — a narrow coastal route worn smooth by centuries of faithful feet. Small offerings lined the way: coins, dried flowers, carved wooden dancers. As they descended into Joybright's eastern residential quarter, the city revealed itself.

This was not a decades-old ruin. This was a crime scene. Festival lanterns torn down and smashed. Temple doors broken open. Bloodstains not yet washed by rain. The Accord had murdered this place days ago, at the same hour they murdered its goddess.

But the city was fighting back. Golden light seeped through cracks in damaged walls. Smashed lanterns flickered with phantom flame. Impossible scarlet flowers pushed up through broken cobblestones. The laughter was there — faint, wounded, heartbroken — but real. Buildings the Accord had smashed refused to stay broken. Cracks filled with warmth. The architecture of joy would not surrender.

Lucian cast detect magic at the entrance and was nearly blinded. The Book of Exultation drowned out everything — evocation magic so intense it filled the space like staring at the sun. But beneath that, quieter: the stones of the city themselves were faintly magical. Residual divine energy soaked into the architecture over centuries of worship. The whole city hummed. And it was responding to the Book.

Lil Moon cast Speak with Plants on the Lastbloom and received not words but feelings — the exhaustion of being the last candle lit in a dark house. The warmth of almost being home after a very long journey. An urgent, desperate, gentle plea: _please._

They moved through the eastern residences. Paul recognized his childhood. A bakery where his mother bought honeycakes. The corner where kids played. A school with its door hanging off hinges. Every smashed window was someone he knew.

They pressed west toward the Sunken Temple. To reach it, they had to cross the garrison's patrol routes.

---

# Three-Way Fight

A Helmite patrol spotted them in the northern streets. Two scared soldiers, swords drawn, voices shaking. The party chose violence. One soldier died.

The city reacted. The temperature dropped. The golden light pulled back. The scarlet flowers closed. The faint laughter went silent. And from the cobblestones around the dead soldier's body, something began to push through — small shapes, pitiful and terrible, born from the grief of murder in a place soaked in fresh death.

The Wretched crawled out of the cracks. A swarm of small horrors shaped like starving children and broken animals — the accumulated petty griefs of a massacred city given tiny, pitiful bodies.

The fight pushed the party west through the streets. The city guided them — golden light tracing a path, doors swinging open, the Lastbloom pulling, the distant laughter growing louder in the direction of the Sunken Temple. Joy's architecture herding them home. Every act of violence in the grief-soaked city birthed more sorrowsworn.

Paul opened the Book. He read. And the golden light erupted like sunrise.

The Sorrowsworn flinched. Staggered. The Lonely's grip loosened. The shadows thinned. Joy — genuine, defiant, chosen in the face of horror — was the weapon that worked. Not swords. Not spells. A man reading a dead goddess's love letter aloud while monsters clawed at his friends.

They fought their way to the temple steps.

---

# The Sunken Temple

Heavy iron doors covered in Helmite wards — four layers of sigils and chains, each one cracked from the inside. Golden light pouring through the fractures. A hum that vibrated in their teeth.

Lucian Misty Stepped through the wards, bypassed them entirely, and opened the doors from inside.

The doors settled shut behind them. The Sorrowsworn couldn't cross the threshold. For one moment — safety.

The temple interior was untouched. The one place in Joybright the Accord couldn't break. Pews draped in scarlet silk. An altar covered in flowers that should have died but hadn't. Candles burning without consuming wax. 

---

# The Convergence

Paul placed the Book of Exultation on the altar. The golden sphere emerged from the nearby lastbloom and moved above the altar. The pages began turning on their own. The temple warmed. And the Book began to drain.

Lliira's words were flowing out of the pages and sinking into the altar stone — centuries of divine authorship pouring into Joybright's foundations. The Book was emptying itself. Giving everything back to the city where it was written.

For the Convergence to complete, the Lastbloom had to join. Voice and ember reunited in the grave. But Lil Moon hesitated. The flower was his. It had chosen him. Placing it on the altar meant letting go.

Lucian stepped forward instead. He had no artifact. No divine connection. But the temple was the thinnest point between planes in the world, and the half-open Convergence had cracked something wider. He reached into the gap — and something reached back.

Not Lliira. Something new. Something unnamed. A spark of raw divine potential that hadn't decided what it was yet. It chose Lucian because he chose himself in a room full of divine artifacts. Everyone else reached for a god's power. Lucian reached for the door handle.

A mark appeared on the back of his left hand. Faint. Warm. Not any symbol he recognized. The beginning of something that had never existed before.

The Sorrowsworn outside were breaking through. The temple's protection was failing. The Lonely's arm punched through the cracked doorframe. Small shapes squeezed through gaps in the stonework. And the individual Sorrowsworn began to merge — the Lonely wrapping around the Lost, the Wretched crawling into the mass — forming something massive. An Amalgamation. The concentrated grief of an entire murdered city coalescing into one terrible shape that filled the doorway.

Lil Moon placed the Lastbloom on the altar.

The flower and the sphere found each other. Ember met voice. The scarlet deepened to something beyond color. The Book stopped draining — the circuit completed. And Lliira's last words wrote themselves in golden ink across the Unwritten Chapter, wet and shining, pouring out as if someone had been holding their breath since the day they died.

The light erupted. Not from the Book — from everything. The altar, the walls, the floor, the painted dancers on the ceiling who seemed to move. A wave of golden-scarlet light rolling outward from the Sunken Temple through the streets of Joybright.

The wave kept going. Through the Festival Square — the fountain running warm and clear. Through the eastern residences — shrine niches filling with golden light. Through the garrison — soldiers dropping weapons because their hands opened.

Across the Mirthwater, fifteen miles east, the lights of Accord's Rest glimmered. Joybright was glowing gold. The capital could see it.

The Book of Exultation would not close. Its pages displayed a different passage to everyone who looked — whatever they needed most. New text appeared on the final page. Not Lliira's handwriting.

Paul's.

# What They Found in the Earth

They went to the Last Festival Ground — the field northwest of the city where only uniform grass grew and divine sensitivity heard distant music. After the Convergence, scarlet flowers crept inward from the edges, asking permission.

They dug at the center where the grass was faintly golden and the earth was body-warm.

She was small. Human-sized. Red hair spread through the soil like roots — still bright, still the color of festival ribbons. Scarlet robes, torn and bloodstained. One wound: Tyr's blade. Not a weapon of cruelty — a weapon of law. Divine judicial authority given killing edge. A verdict made physical.

She hadn't decomposed. The earth preserved her. Joy doesn't rot.

Her hands were open. Palms up. Like she'd been offering something when she fell.

They mended her — Paul reading from the Book while others held head to body. The divine voice and divine flesh recognized each other. But it was not enough to mend her head to her body.

They buried her properly. In the earth that had protected her. The scarlet flowers would cover the grave in time.

From her body they recovered relics of a dead goddess: a crystallized tear the size of a thumbnail, a shard of dark red crystal from Tyr's killing wound, and dozens of clay festival tokens the earth had gathered around her like a blanket.

Karsa skinned her with the shard that killed her. Because Karsa was always what he was — brutal, practical, and operating by a morality no one else in the party shared.

---

# The Sanctuary and the Schism

They returned to the Sunken Temple to find Pilgrims had arrived — followers of Lliira drawn by the golden light, refugees from Greyshore, the faithful who had been hiding and praying in secret. They filled the pews. They wept. They sang. The first voices raised in Joybright since the city was murdered.

Lucian charged them entry fees.

Paul — the only genuine follower of Lliira left, the man the Book had chosen, the man who had read a goddess's last words aloud while monsters clawed at the doors — looked at what was happening in the temple. The commerce. The skin of his goddess draped over a warrior's shoulders. The casual desecration dressed as practicality.

He banished Karsa from the city. Karsa, wearing the skin of Lliira, forced out of Joybright but did not look back. Water Walt soon followed, angered by the departure of his captain. 

Paul stayed. The Book rested open on the altar. The Lastbloom settled into the temple like a candle finding its sconce. The city hummed. The laughter was steady now, not ghostly — present, real, alive. Joybright had a keeper.

Lucian snuck away and Lil Moon parted with some final words of respect to Paul. They all met outside the city. They carried relics of a dead goddess, a divine mark that hadn't decided what it was yet.

Behind them, Joybright glowed gold against the evening sky. A sanctuary. A beacon. A target.

That evening they met a traveling merchant band who was bound for Joybright. Lucian stopped them right outside the city. After some discussion they decided the best course of action was to avoid the city and follow the accords road to helmgate. Bound for briarholt.
